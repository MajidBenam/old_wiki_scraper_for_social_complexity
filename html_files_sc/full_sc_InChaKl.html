♠ Polity Population ♣ [7,000,000-8,000,000]: 1000 CE; [7,500,000-8,500,000]: 1100 CE ♥ People.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]ET: By 200 BC 30 million on the Indian Subcontinent, 20 million (40%) in Ganges basin. "The next fifteen hundred years consolidated without significantly altering this pattern."[MAJIDBENAM_REF_19_InChaKl] McEvedy and Jones estimated for Pakistan, India and Bangladesh 77m for 1000 CE, 80m for 1100 CE. If the proportion within the Ganges basin remained the same (40%) that leaves for the rest of the Indian Subcontinent: 46.2m for 1000 CE, 48 for 1100 CE. Pakistan contains the Indus valley which presumably also was densely populated. If we assume the fertile Indus valley contained the majority (50% population?) of the remaining population, whilst respecting the claim that "the demographic centre of the country" was the Gangetic provinces (so Indus probably does not hold much more than 50% of the non-Gangetic population) that leaves for the remaining areas: 23.1m for 1000 CE, 24m for 1100 CE. The remaining area left covers 2,000,000 km2 and the polity of about 650,000 KM2 covered about 33% of this area. So, assuming an even distribution of population across the remaining landmass, a population magnitude estimate would be: 7.6m for 1000 CE, 7.9m for 1100 CE. According to maps of 800-900 CE[MAJIDBENAM_REF_20_InChaKl] there were about 6-7 other polities in the remaining region during the same time period.[MAJIDBENAM_BR]

Law

♠ Formal legal code ♣ present ♥ The "judicial [...] administration of the Chalukyas resembled that of their ancestors" [MAJIDBENAM_REF_49_InChaKl]: therefore, like the Chalukyas of Badami, they must have followed smirtis and dharmashastras [MAJIDBENAM_REF_50_InChaKl]. The Smriti, or Manu-smriti, is a collection of texts prescribing correct behaviour, including a section explicitly devoted to "the law of kings" [MAJIDBENAM_REF_51_InChaKl], while the dharmashastras are a collection of more explicitly legal texts [MAJIDBENAM_REF_52_InChaKl].
♠ Judges ♣ present ♥ The "judicial [...] administration of the Chalukyas resembled that of their ancestors" [MAJIDBENAM_REF_53_InChaKl], the Chalukyas of Badami, for whom the Emperor was the supreme judge, but he also dispensed justice through judges he himself appointed [MAJIDBENAM_REF_54_InChaKl].
