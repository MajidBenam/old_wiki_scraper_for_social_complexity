♠ Duration ♣ 300 BCE-100 BCE ♥ The Zapotec polity began to expand at the beginning of this phase, conquering the Cañada de Cuicatlán and other areas.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_1_MxAlb1L][MAJIDBENAM_REF_2_MxAlb1L]
♠ Degree of centralization ♣ [confederation; unitary state] ♥ The establishment of a confederation (although not yet a state) at the beginning of Monte Albán Early I is suggested by the simultaneous abandonment of settlements and founding of Monte Albán, which itself was internally divided into different groupings of people, based on distinctions of pottery assemblages.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_3_MxAlb1L] At the beginning of this phase (Late I), the people at Monte Albán began to expand beyond the valley and conquered Cañada de Cuicatlán (as well as other areas including: Ejutla, Peñoles, Miahuatlán and the San Francisco Arriba area)[MAJIDBENAM_REF_4_MxAlb1L], which remained in a subordinate, possibly tributary, relationship with Monte Albán until around 200 CE (the end of the Monte Albán II phase).[MAJIDBENAM_REF_5_MxAlb1L]
♠ Supra-polity relations ♣ alliance ♥ It has been argued that the Zapotecs at Monte Alban and the Teotihuacan state built mutually peaceful relationships, while both states were more powerful than other neighbouring polities. Neither conquered the other (based on distinct material culture and architectural styles) but did trade with one another.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_6_MxAlb1L] Monte Alban Late I: “The expansionist tactics adopted by the rulers of Monte Albán apparently took many forms—including not just conquest, but also colonization, episodic raiding, the establishment of marriage alliances and/or trading relationships (perhaps backed by threats or occasional military force), and co-option of existing political structures.”[MAJIDBENAM_REF_7_MxAlb1L]

♠ Capital ♣ Monte Albán ♥ Monte Albán was the largest city in the Zapotec polity at this time and had an estimated 17,242 inhabitants (or one third of the entire population of the valley).[MAJIDBENAM_REF_12_MxAlb1L]
♠ Language ♣ Zapotec ♥ “…the iconography and hieroglyphic writing of Monte Albán I suggest that we are dealing with people who spoke an early version of Zapotec and practiced an early form of Zapotec religion.”[MAJIDBENAM_REF_13_MxAlb1L]
♠ Linguistic Family ♣ Otomanguean ♥ Both the Zapotecan (Zapotec and Chatino) and Mixtecan (Mixtec, Cuicatec and Trique) languages belong to the Otomanguean language family, which also includes: Otopamean, Chinantecan Popolocan, Huave, Amuzgo and Manguean language groups.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_14_MxAlb1L]

♠ Religion Sect ♣ ♥The Monte Albán Late I period ran from 300 to 100 BCE. Archaeologists Charles Spencer and Elsa Redmond argue that the Zapotecs had formed a state by the beginning of this period (around 300 BCE).[MAJIDBENAM_REF_15_MxAlb1L] The Zapotec polity based at Monte Albán started to expand to areas outside the valley at the beginning of this period, but could not yet control all areas within the Valley of Oaxaca.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_16_MxAlb1L] It is thought that the unification of the whole valley by the Zapotec state did not occur until the following period (Monte Albán II).[MAJIDBENAM_REF_17_MxAlb1L][MAJIDBENAM_BR]Population and political organization[MAJIDBENAM_BR]There is evidence of an elite during this period. However, the nature of government is still unclear, as is the relationship between elites and non-elites.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_18_MxAlb1L] Richard Blanton et al. summarize the situation: 'Obviously, powerful authorities at Monte Albán could collect taxes, wage wars, engage in diplomacy, erect carved stone monuments, construct public buildings, manage urban problems, appropriate surpluses from producer households, and adjudicate disputes. But who were these authorities and how did they come to positions of power? Were there rulers or a governing council?'[MAJIDBENAM_REF_19_MxAlb1L][MAJIDBENAM_BR]Violent conflicts were rife, and one third of the population of the valley eventually moved to fortified or defensible settlements by the end of Monte Albán I (including Monte Albán itself, which had a three-kilometre defensive wall and was built on a defensible hill top).[MAJIDBENAM_REF_20_MxAlb1L] The danzantes, or carved images of captives, date to the first Monte Albán phases (MA Early-Late I) and may be linked with the expansionist policies and propaganda of Zapotec leaders. It is also worth noting that the richest burials date to this period and, like the danzantes, may be linked with an expression and justification of power by Zapotec elites.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_21_MxAlb1L][MAJIDBENAM_BR]The population of the Zapotec polity grew during this period, particularly at Monte Albán, which housed around 17,000 people, and many new settlements were established within a roughly 30-kilometre radius of Monte Albán.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_22_MxAlb1L] This was part of a general trend of population nucleation during this period, particularly to sites with civic-ceremonial functions.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_23_MxAlb1L] The entire population of the Valley of Oaxaca during the Monte Albán IC phase has been estimated at around 50,000,[MAJIDBENAM_REF_24_MxAlb1L] although it is not clear whether the entire valley was under Zapotec control at this time.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_25_MxAlb1L]
