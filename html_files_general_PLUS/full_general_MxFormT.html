♠ Duration ♣ 100 BCE-99 CE ♥ [MAJIDBENAM_REF_1_MxFormT] The following refers to a previous periodization. The start date 650 BC for the MxFormT quasi-polity is the beginning of the Late Formative period (c.650-200 BC) in the Basin of Mexico (alternatively called "First Intermediate Period 2").[MAJIDBENAM_REF_2_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_3_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_4_MxFormT] The end date is fuzzy and problematic because it is unclear exactly when in the Terminal Formative period (c.200-1 BC; alternatively called "First Intermediate Period 3") the entire MxFormT quasi-polity was conquered by its aggressive neighbors Cuicuilco (Basin of Mexico-Cui) and Teotihuacan (Basin of Mexico-Teo). Since the MxFormT quasi-polity is made up of multiple, discrete, independent settlement clusters, the timing of their conquest by Cuicuilco and Teotihuacan was most likely different for different areas.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_5_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_6_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_7_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_8_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_9_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_10_MxFormT] This quasi-polity ceased to exist during the subsequent Tzacualli ceramic phase (c.1 BCE - 100 CE) because the Plinian eruption of Popocatepetl led to the abandonment of most of the quasi-polity', while the rest of the quasi-polity was taken over by Teotihuacan and Cuicuilco.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_11_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_12_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_13_MxFormT] The date ranges for the eruption/abandonment are c. 100 BCE - 50 CE, and the start date for the Tzacualli ceramic transition is centered around 1 BCE, so I use the date of 1 BCE to designate both events.

♠ Supra-polity relations ♣ suspected unknown ♥ The settlement clusters of the MxFormT quasi-polity are thought to have been relatively independent and autonomous chiefdom-level polities during the Late Formative, but then gradually came under the control of neighboring Cuicuilco and Teotihuacan during the Terminal Formative c.200-1 BCE. While some have hypothesized that Cuicuilco headed some kind of Supra-polity political system during the Late Formative,[MAJIDBENAM_REF_14_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_15_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_16_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_17_MxFormT] the characteristics of such a Supra-polity political system are unknown to archaeologists.

♠ Linguistic family ♣ Otomanguean; Mixe-Zoquean ♥ The Basin of Mexico was a cultural crossroads during the Formative and Classic Periods, and their material culture has clear ties to both east (Puebla-Tlaxcalla, Eastern Moreos, Gulf Coast) and west (Michoacan/Guanajuato, Toluca Valley, Western Morelos). The (Proto-)Oto-Manguean language family spread northward into the NGA with the first farming populations, and there is cultural continuity throughout the Formative.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_18_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_19_MxFormT] Nevertheless, there were also considerable Olmec stylistic influences in the Basin of Mexico and Morelos (Chalcatzingo) during the Early-Middle Formative, which may indicate intrusive Mixe-Zoquean speakers.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_20_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_21_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_22_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_23_MxFormT] Due to the massive population movement from the Formative to the time of the Spanish Contact -- including the widespread displacement of local ethnic groups by Nahuatl-speaking Uto-Aztecans -- and the lack of a deciphered writing system in the Basin of Mexico NGA prior to Nahuatl, the language(s) of the MxFormT remain unknown.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_24_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_25_MxFormT] The material/symbolic culture of the MxFormT quasi-polity has strong links with the occupants of the Puebla-Tlaxcala Valley to the east, and later Teotihuacan (the so-called "Olmeca-Xicallanca" ethnie), and it has been suggested that these peoples spoke variants of Mixe-Zoquean and/or Totonac languages from the Gulf Coast (based on loan words in local Nahuat dialects).[MAJIDBENAM_REF_26_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_27_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_28_MxFormT] On the other hand, the local red-on-buff pottery tradition is shared across west-central Mexico, and there are clear stylistic and material culture ties between Cuicuilco and the Chupicuaro basin of southeast Guanajuato,[MAJIDBENAM_REF_29_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_30_MxFormT] which would suggest Oto-Pamean or proto-Purépecha/Tarrascan languages.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_31_MxFormT] Given the great miscegenation of cultural influences in the area, it is likely that multiple languages were spoken, likely leading to unique new cultures, languages, and ethnicities.

[MAJIDBENAM_BR]The Basin or Valley of Mexico is a highlands plateau in central Mexico roughly corresponding to modern-day Mexico City. Here, we are interested in the phase of its prehistory known as the Terminal Formative period (c. 100 BCE-99 CE). The most notable aspect of this period is the emergence of Teotihuacan as the largest and most populous city in the New World: by 150 CE, it had a population ranging between 60,000 to 80,000 inhabitants distributed across an area of about 20 kilometers.[MAJIDBENAM_REF_32_MxFormT] [MAJIDBENAM_BR]Political power was inherently theocratic;[MAJIDBENAM_REF_33_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_34_MxFormT][MAJIDBENAM_REF_35_MxFormT] beyond that, the exact administrative mechanisms prevalent at the time remain unclear.
