General Description

The Northern Song (or Sung) Dynasty was a period of great economic advancement, population growth, urbanization, and political change in China.[13] Eleventh-century China under the Song has been called the 'most advanced place in the world' at that time.[14] The Northern Song government ruled from its capital in Kaifeng, while the Southern Song were based in Hangzhou.
The century between the fall of the Tang dynasty and beginning of the Song dynasty was characterized by the rise of powerful warlords in the south and political turmoil in the north.[15] The first Song emperor, Emperor Taizu, reunified the Yangtze River Valley and South China.[16] The territory held by the Song was smaller than that held by previous powerful dynasties, and much of North China was still dominated by outside rule.[17]

Population and political organization

The Song government was marked by the increasing importance of the civil service examination and the rise of Confucianism.[18] The prominence of the civil service examination led to the emergence of a central government governed by scholar-officials rather than by aristocrats, as was traditionally the case. The government was headed by a powerful emperor and featured a large central bureaucracy.[19]
The Song period saw rapid commercial and industrial expansion.[20] Agriculture, paper-making, printing, and iron-working flourished,[21][22] and paper money was first produced in China under the Song.[23] Foreign trade increased as the state expanded its trading networks.[24]
Although it is clear that the Song Dynasty was a period of massive population growth and urbanization,[25] there is some disagreement about exact population numbers. Some scholars agree that the population had reached around 100 million by 1000 CE,[26][27] while others believe it was closer to 60 million.[28]

♠ Military levels ♣ [8-10] ♥ levels.

"Several innovations in the Song army during the Tangut war transformed the army's structure, armament and tactics. The most important change was in the creation of "legions" (jiang) of from 2,500 to 4,000 men (on rare occasions as high as 10,000 men) as permanent maneuver elements. The success of the legions in the latter stages of the war not only discredited the older system, in which armies were created by drawing disparate battalions of 500 men from large garrisons, but also created a distinction between field or border forces and the garrison forces in the capital. The legion was more formally introduced throughout the army during the New Policies reforms of Wang Anshi during the reign of Song Shenzong (1067-1085)."[106]
